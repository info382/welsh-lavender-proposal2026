Snapshot

Website and being found

When someone discovers the farm online, the first impression is currently weighted towards skincare, retail and visits. That makes sense historically, but it does mean the events opportunity sits slightly further into the journey.

For organisers actively searching for an event space, that extra step can sometimes be the difference between enquiring or moving on.

On the Special Events page, you already communicate the right direction: board meetings, corporate events, retreats and away days, with capacity up to 30 guests, supported by optional workshops and catering. That's a strong base and gives people a sense of possibility.

The next step here isn't more content — it's clarity. Helping the right organisers understand quickly:

  • what the space is best suited for
  • what's realistic
  • and whether it's the right fit for them

That naturally reduces back-and-forth and protects time for a small team.

Product clarity: what are we really selling?

In practice, "corporate events" covers very different needs.

Two common types come up again and again:

  • Corporate meetings and strategy days, where people care about Wi-Fi, layout, privacy, timings and logistics
  • Away days and retreats, where people care more about flow, experience, activities, food and atmosphere

Because away days and retreats align so naturally with the farm's setting, they're a strong candidate to become the hero offer early on — supported by a small number of pre-designed formats that are easy to understand and easy to price confidently.

Board meetings can still sit alongside this, but they don't need to carry the whole events strategy from the start.

Cost, delivery and early pressure points

At this stage, the biggest risk isn't lack of interest — it's underestimating the true cost and effort behind delivering events.

Even small events quietly include: staff time, setup and reset, cleaning, utilities, supplier coordination, risk management, and often a lot of email back-and-forth.

Getting three things clear early makes a huge difference:

  1. what's included and what isn't
  2. the real cost to deliver each type of event
  3. a booking journey that filters enquiries before they reach your inbox

That's what keeps events rewarding rather than draining.

Data: small insights that reduce guesswork

Because the events side is new, early data is incredibly useful.

Simple visibility on:

  • where enquiries come from
  • how people move through the website
  • which pages prompt contact
  • and where people hesitate

quickly shows what's working and what needs adjusting — without overcomplicating things.

1. Product

Defining the experience — and pricing it with confidence

Before marketing works, and before pricing feels solid, the product itself needs clarity.

Here, "product" doesn't mean forcing everything into rigid packages. It means clearly defining what kind of experience you're offering, who it's best suited for, and how it's meant to be delivered.

At the moment, the farm can host:

  • board meetings and structured corporate sessions
  • retreats and away days
  • workshops and hosted experiences

Each of these carries different expectations, costs and levels of effort.

Part of my role is to help you:

  • decide which event types to prioritise first
  • understand which ones are more complex to deliver
  • and shape the offer so it works commercially, not just creatively

For example, predesigned away-day formats are one strong option — not because everything must be packaged, but because they:

  • reduce long back-and-forth with organisers
  • make value easier to communicate
  • allow pricing to be based on the experience, not just numbers
  • align naturally with the unique setting of the farm

Crucially, pricing here doesn't come from comparing yourself to other venues or charging "per head" by default. It comes from understanding real cost + value of the space.

I can support you in:

  • mapping costs per delegate vs per group
  • understanding the real cost of catering, staff time, facilities, setup and reset
  • accounting for contracts, admin time and risk
  • identifying both the small and hidden costs that affect margin

This allows pricing decisions to be made based on facts, not instinct — and adjusted confidently when needed.

2. Operations

Building a clear journey, reducing risk, and protecting margins

Operations is where many event businesses quietly struggle — not because demand isn't there, but because the journey from enquiry to delivery isn't clearly designed.

My focus here is helping you build a clear, intelligent visitor and organiser journey, from first contact through to booking, delivery and follow-up — so nothing relies on guesswork or endless back-and-forth.

A key part of this is how enquiries are handled.

Rather than everything landing as open emails, I can support you in shaping:

  • enquiry forms that feel thoughtful and enjoyable for the client
  • questions that help people clarify what they actually need
  • intelligent forms that give you insight before you even reply

Good enquiry forms don't feel transactional — they feel like the beginning of the experience. At the same time, they quietly filter out unsuitable requests and save hours of admin.

From there, support can include:

  • automated but human-sounding responses that set expectations early
  • a clear path from enquiry → proposal → booking
  • reducing repetitive email conversations
  • making it easier for the team to prioritise the most suitable and profitable enquiries

Launch & Opening Strategy

Using the opening to create momentum, not pressure

With the events space opening soon, the launch itself is a valuable opportunity — not just to show the space, but to shape how it is perceived from the start.

Rather than a simple open day, the opening can be planned as a curated, invitation-led event that introduces the farm to the right people and sets expectations clearly.

Support here can include:

  • defining the purpose of the launch (who it's for and why)
  • shaping the flow of the day so guests understand the experience
  • being intentional about who is invited (organisers, facilitators, partners, decision-makers)
  • planning how the space is used and experienced
  • ensuring the team knows what's happening, when, and why

A well-planned launch can:

  • create early advocates and word-of-mouth
  • generate useful feedback and content
  • lead directly to future conversations and bookings
  • reduce the need for heavy promotion later

The aim is to create clarity and confidence from day one — for guests and for the team.

Strategy & Delivery

Running Events on the Ground

Why planning is what makes events calm, safe and cost-effective

Alongside strategy and planning, I can also support running events on the ground where it makes sense.

I've delivered over 300 corporate events, ranging from small leadership sessions to large-scale events of 200+ people to a royal visit and a wedding — often with very small teams, and sometimes entirely on my own.

That isn't about doing everything myself. It's about planning events so they don't need constant staffing, firefighting or decision-making on the day.

In my experience, the biggest cost and stress in events doesn't come from the event itself — it comes from poor planning. For example:

  • designing self-service coffee and tea stations so no one needs to stand and pour drinks
  • setting up catering and buffet flows that require minimal supervision
  • hiring equipment strategically (such as plates or glassware) and passing the cost through, rather than washing, storing and managing stock
  • planning alcohol service so it is largely self-managed, with staff stepping in only where needed
  • clearly directing guests through the day so people aren't wandering and the team always knows what's happening next

When the flow of the day is clear — from arrival, through sessions, breaks and transitions — staffing needs drop dramatically.

Overstaffing often happens when an event hasn't been fully thought through. When people don't know what to do, you end up paying for uncertainty.

Good planning means: fewer people needed, clearer roles, calmer delivery, lower staffing costs, and a better experience for guests.

Compliance, safety and responsible delivery

Health, safety and compliance are part of this planning — not an afterthought.

Responsible event delivery means ensuring the right roles and responsibilities are in place, including:

  • first aid cover where required
  • fire safety planning and fire marshal responsibilities
  • food handling requirements and appropriate certification through caterers or team members
  • compliant alcohol service, with clear controls and supervision

I have previously held first aid, fire marshal and food safety training, and I also hold a personal alcohol licence, which allows me to understand and manage alcohol service responsibly within an event setting.

Where certifications need to be current or held by specific team members, I help plan this clearly so responsibilities are shared appropriately, the team feels supported, and the farm is protected from unnecessary risk.

If needed, I can:

  • plan the full run-of-show
  • brief the team clearly
  • direct the event on the day
  • and step in where experience matters most

This allows the team to focus on hospitality and care, rather than reacting to problems.

When events are planned this way, they feel calm, professional and intentional — and they cost significantly less to deliver than people expect.
